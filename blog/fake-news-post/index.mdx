<p>Is it legit?</p>
<p>Five steps for vetting a news source
Many sources compete for attention online, including partisan blogs and bogus sites posing as legitimate news organizations. It can be toughto know what to trust. Follow these steps to cut through the noise and learn how to evaluate sources for signs of credibility - as well as for red flags that signal a source should be avoided.</p>
<p>1. Do a quick search
Conducting a simple search for information about a news source is a key first step in evaluating its credibility it's important to lock beyond social media. Go to a search engine and plug in the name of the website or publication. Do other legitimate sources, such at standards-based news organizations or fact-checking websites, describe this publication as unreliable? Satirical? Is it a state-run propaganda "news" site? If so, there's no need to spend more time vetting. Look elsewhere for reliable information.</p>
<p>2.Look for standards
Reputable news organization aspire to ethical guidelines and standards, including falmess, accuracy and independence. These standards should be avaliable publicly - often in the form of editorial policies or a code of ethics. Look for evidence that a source follows such standards. If someone is mentioned in a cricla story, for instance, did the journalist give that person a chance to comment?

Note: Some professional jounalism standards are commonly shared acreess the industry, such as the Society or Professional Journalists Code of Ethics. Other policies are unique to different newsrooms.</p>

<p>3. Check för transparency
Quality news sources should be transparent, not only about their reporting practices see step 2 , but also about their ownership and funding. Is it clear who owns and runs this website or publication? Is advetising labeled responsibly? Can you tell who is writing and producing content? Is there a way to contact newsroom editors and reporters?

Remember:"About" pages can provide some of these details, but they can also be misleading and omit important information (about a source's ownership or lack of independence, for example): Don't be fooled by a sleed web design, or trus the "About" page on a unfamilar site without further verification. If something seems suspicious, check it out.</p>

<p>4. Examine how errors are handled
Credible news sources are accountable for mistakes and correct them. Do you see evidence that this source corrects or clarifies errors?</p>

<p>5 Assess news coverage
An important step in vetting sources is taking time to read and assess severalnews articles: Do you see original reporting? Do they provide straight news reports, or just commentary and opinion? are  there grammatical and/or spelling errors? How do news articles from this source compare to coverage from other standards-based newsrooms on this same topic? Many people have strong opinions about news sources without evaluating them firsthand.</p>

<h3>Beware of these trust busters!</h3>
<p>If you spot any of these problems, lock elsewhere for credible news.</p>
<p>* False or untrue content:
Publishing a demonstrably false claim without correcting it</p>
<p>* Clickbait tactics:
"Bating" you with sensationalized language, including missleading or exaggerated headlines, or headlines that are purposely vague to drive clicks</p>
<p>* Lack of balans
Consistently focusing on one angle or side of a legitimate degate or controversy, while ignoring or distorting other perspectives</p>
<p>* Manipulated images or videos
Tip: You can do a reverse image search using GOOGLE - TINEYE - YANDEX
(among other tools to see if visuals have been misrepresented or altered in same way</p>
<p>* State-run or state-sponsored propaganda;
Using government-controlled news organizations to further national agendas an improve public image</p>
<p>* Dangerous, offensive and malicious content:
Could include discriminatory language, unverified pseudoscience, content that promotes violence for corverage that - as the SPJ Code o Ethics states -- pander "to lurid curiosity"</p>
